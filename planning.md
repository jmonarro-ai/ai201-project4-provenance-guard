# Provenance Guard — Planning Document

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: Groq LLM Classification
- **What it measures:** Semantic and stylistic coherence — whether the text reads as AI-generated based on phrasing patterns, structural uniformity, generic transitions, and absence of personal voice
- **Output format:** A float score between 0.0 and 1.0, where 1.0 = high confidence AI-generated, 0.0 = high confidence human-written
- **Why it differs between human/AI:** AI models produce smooth, coherent, well-structured text. Human writing contains idiosyncrasies, tangents, informal phrasing, and personality that LLMs rarely replicate naturally
- **Blind spot:** Highly polished human writing (academic papers, professional essays, non-native English speakers writing formally) can be misclassified as AI. Also struggles with heavily human-edited AI text

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics
- **What it measures:** Three statistical/structural properties of the text:
  1. **Sentence length variance** — standard deviation of sentence lengths in words. Human writing varies more; AI writing is more uniform
  2. **Type-token ratio (TTR)** — unique words divided by total words. Human writing uses more diverse vocabulary; AI writing reuses common words more
  3. **Punctuation density** — punctuation marks per word. Human writing uses more informal punctuation (dashes, ellipses, exclamation points); AI is more conservative
- **Output format:** Each metric is computed and normalized to 0.0–1.0, then averaged into a single stylometric score where 1.0 = AI-like uniformity, 0.0 = human-like irregularity
- **Why it differs between human/AI:** AI generates statistically uniform text. Human writing has natural irregularity in rhythm, sentence structure, and word choice
- **Blind spot:** Formal human writing (legal documents, academic writing, technical manuals) is naturally uniform and will score similarly to AI text. Cannot capture meaning — only structure

### Combining Signals into a Confidence Score
- **Weighted average:** `confidence = (llm_score * 0.60) + (style_score * 0.40)`
- Groq LLM is weighted higher (60%) because it captures semantic meaning, which is a stronger signal than surface statistics
- Stylometrics is weighted at 40% as a structural cross-check that is independent of the LLM's judgment
- Both scores are always returned individually in the API response alongside the combined score

## Uncertainty Representation

### What Confidence Scores Mean
- A score of **0.90+** means both signals strongly agree the content is AI-generated — high confidence
- A score of **0.70–0.89** means the signals lean toward AI but with meaningful uncertainty
- A score of **0.35–0.69** means signals are mixed or weak — the system genuinely cannot determine attribution confidently
- A score of **0.15–0.34** means signals lean toward human-written but with some uncertainty
- A score of **0.0–0.14** means both signals strongly agree the content is human-written — high confidence

### Label Thresholds
| Score Range | Attribution Result | Label Variant |
|---|---|---|
| 0.70 – 1.00 | likely_ai | High-Confidence AI |
| 0.26 – 0.69 | uncertain | Uncertain |
| 0.00 – 0.25 | likely_human | High-Confidence Human |
### What 0.6 Means
A confidence score of 0.60 means the system leans toward AI-generated but is not confident enough to say so definitively. The label will communicate uncertainty clearly and remind the reader that the creator can appeal.

## Transparency Label Design

All three label variants are written in plain language. No technical jargon. A non-technical reader must understand what each label means.

### Variant 1: High-Confidence AI (score ≥ 0.85)
⚠️ Likely AI-Generated

Our system has analyzed this content and found strong indicators that it was

produced by an AI writing tool. This label is based on automated analysis and

may not be perfect. If you are the creator and believe this is incorrect,

you can submit an appeal to have your work reviewed by a person.

Confidence: High

### Variant 2: Uncertain (score 0.35–0.84)
🔍 Attribution Uncertain

Our system analyzed this content but could not confidently determine whether

it was written by a person or generated by AI. This sometimes happens with

formal writing styles or edited content. The creator can submit an appeal

to provide additional context.

Confidence: Low — treat this label with caution

### Variant 3: High-Confidence Human (score ≤ 0.14)

✅ Likely Human-Written

Our system analyzed this content and found strong indicators that it was

written by a person. This label is based on automated analysis. No system

is perfect — if you have concerns about this content, you can flag it for review.

Confidence: High

## Appeals Workflow

### Who Can Appeal
Any creator who submitted content and received a content_id can submit an appeal. No authentication system is implemented — the content_id serves as the identifier.

### What They Provide
- `content_id` — the unique ID returned when they submitted their content
- `creator_reasoning` — a plain text explanation of why they believe the classification is wrong

### What the System Does When an Appeal Is Received
1. Looks up the original submission in the audit log by content_id
2. Updates the content's status from `"classified"` to `"under_review"`
3. Writes a new audit log entry that includes:
   - The original attribution result and confidence score
   - The appeal reasoning provided by the creator
   - A timestamp of when the appeal was filed
   - Status field set to `"under_review"`
4. Returns a confirmation JSON response to the creator

### What a Human Reviewer Sees
When a reviewer calls GET /log, they see entries with `"status": "under_review"` and a populated `appeal_reasoning` field alongside the original classification data. This gives them everything needed to make a fair judgment.

## Anticipated Edge Cases

### Edge Case 1: Non-Native English Speaker Writing Formally
A non-native English speaker may write in an unusually formal, uniform style — short sentences, common vocabulary, conservative punctuation — because they are being careful. Both signals will score this as AI-like. The stylometric signal will flag low variance and low TTR; the LLM may interpret formal phrasing as AI-generated. This is the most likely source of false positives in this system.

### Edge Case 2: Heavily Edited AI Output
A creator who generates text with AI and then substantially rewrites it will produce content that has human-like irregularity in structure but may retain AI-like semantic patterns. The stylometric signal may score it as human-written while the LLM scores it as AI-generated. The confidence score will land in the uncertain range (0.35–0.65), which is actually the correct outcome — the system should acknowledge it cannot tell.

### Edge Case 3: Very Short Submissions
A submission of fewer than 30 words gives the stylometric signal almost no data to work with. Sentence length variance and TTR are unreliable at very short lengths. The system should treat these with low confidence regardless of scores.

### Narrative
When a creator submits text via POST /submit, it passes through two independent detection signals — a Groq LLM classifier and a stylometric heuristics analyzer — whose scores are combined into a single weighted confidence score. That score drives a transparency label generator which produces one of three plain-language labels, and the full decision is written to a structured audit log before the response is returned. When a creator submits an appeal via POST /appeal, the system looks up their original submission by content_id, updates its status to under_review, logs the appeal reasoning alongside the original decision, and returns a confirmation.
